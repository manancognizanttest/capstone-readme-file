[Choose one: Good morning. / Good afternoon.] We are Yashwanth Venkatesan and Manan Nayak, and this is our ParaBank QA and Quality Engineering capstone.

Our theme is evidence-driven testing. A high pass count sounds reassuring, but it is only useful when we can explain what was tested, how it was checked, and where the evidence stops. Today we will show the customer risks we prioritized, how our manual and automated layers worked together, the results and defects we found, and the limits created by a shared demo environment. Our goal is not to claim perfect quality. It is to present a traceable and honest quality assessment.

SLIDE 2 — WHAT WE TESTED (target 1:10)

ParaBank was our primary system. It provides both a browser interface and service endpoints, so we could compare behavior across layers.

We prioritized registration and login because they control identity and access. We covered account overview and opening an account because customers must be able to see and manage their accounts. We also covered transfers and transaction history because money movement carries greater business impact than a cosmetic problem.

At the API layer, ParaBank supplied customer, account, transfer, and transaction workflows. We used JSONPlaceholder only for simulated, nonpersistent PUT and DELETE operations; it was support coverage, not part of ParaBank.

Our accessibility review examined the public login-to-registration flow. It included keyboard use, visible focus, one sampled contrast observation, and programmatic labels. It was not a broad WCAG conformance audit. The hosted environment also limited our control over data resets and shared state.

For cross-browser coverage, we manually ran the valid-login flow in Chrome 149 and Edge 150.

SLIDE 3 — HOW WE TESTED IT (target 1:10)

No single tool answers every quality question, so we used several layers with distinct purposes.

Manual testing gave us broad functional, negative, cross-platform, and accessibility coverage. Postman automated API requests, JavaScript assertions, and chained data, so values such as customer and account IDs could flow into later requests. Selenium WebDriver, Java, JUnit 5, and Page Objects protected repeatable browser journeys while keeping locators separate from test intent.

Cucumber expressed login behavior in business-readable form. Its four executed scenario instances came from one normal valid-login scenario plus a Scenario Outline run with three invalid-login example rows. JMeter added a deliberately small, read-only performance baseline. Accessibility checks added the perspective of people navigating by keyboard or assistive technology.

The value is not the number of tools. Each layer answered a different risk question, and the stored reports made the conclusions auditable.

The reconciled formal set contains 22 cases: 11 API, 6 UI, 2 accessibility, and 3 cross-platform. The recorded outcome is 21 PASS and 1 PARTIAL.

SLIDE 5 — DEFECTS WE REPORTED (target 1:10)

We documented three defects and kept observed evidence separate from simulation.

DEF-002 is an observed Medium defect. Registration inputs had nearby visible text, but no associated label element or ARIA accessible name. Sighted keyboard users may understand the form, while screen-reader users may encounter textboxes without an accessible name.

DEF-003 is an observed Low defect. A nonnumeric transfer amount returned HTTP 404. The request was rejected and no successful-transfer message appeared, but we propose HTTP 400 as the clearer validation contract. We are not saying the application returned 400.

DEF-001 is a simulated Medium defect in which registration accepts a whitespace-only First Name. It was not executed or observed; it demonstrates a realistic input-validation risk without being presented as product evidence.

That gives two Medium reports and one Low report, but only DEF-002 and DEF-003 are observed findings.

SLIDE 7 — WHAT WE LEARNED (target 0:55)

Our biggest engineering challenge was repeatable test data. In a hosted demo, accounts can reset, generated users can collide, and one test can depend on state created earlier. A correct test can therefore become unreliable when setup is unclear.

Next, we would generate unique users, create required accounts through controlled setup, record the identifiers used by each run, and clean up where the environment allows it. We would also preserve final machine-readable reports and screenshots together, retain environment details for Chrome and Edge, and broaden accessibility testing into authenticated pages before making wider claims.

The main lesson was that traceability is part of engineering: every visible total needs a real report, observation, or clearly stated qualification.

SLIDE 8 — CONCLUSION (target 0:55)

To conclude, we exercised high-risk customer journeys through UI and API testing and supported them with repeatable automation, BDD, a small performance baseline, and focused accessibility checks.

The formal record is 21 PASS and 1 PARTIAL. That is a strong result within the tested scope, but the accessibility gap, shared-environment constraints, and limited cross-platform evidence prevent a claim of zero risk or universal quality.

Our final takeaway is simple: credible QA pairs evidence with honest limits. Thank you for your time. We welcome your questions.

JMETER DESIGN AND DASHBOARD GUIDE

Design: one read-only GET for customer accounts; 5 threads; 10-second ramp-up; 2 loops per thread; 10 total samples. The current JMX asserts HTTP 200, an XML content type, and response data containing the `<accounts` root. The small workload protects the shared public demo and provides only an educational baseline.

Dashboard terms in plain language:

- Average: all response times added together and divided by the number of samples.
- Median: the midpoint of sorted response times, derived from the two middle values for an even sample count; half are at or below it and half are at or above it.
- Minimum: the fastest recorded response.
- Maximum: the slowest recorded response.
- Error rate: the percentage of samples JMeter marked unsuccessful.
- Throughput: how many requests completed per second during the run.
- Percentile: the time at or below which a stated percentage of samples completed. In this tiny historical run, the 90th percentile was 876.4 ms and the 95th and 99th were 952 ms; ten samples are too few for stable percentile conclusions.

LIKELY Q&A

Q: Why did Postman replace pytest?
A: The project team reported that pytest did not work on the intended work laptop, so Postman became the selected alternative. That is user-provided project context, not a conclusion inferred from the execution artifacts.

Q: Why use JSONPlaceholder?
A: It supplied stable, simulated PUT and DELETE examples. Its writes are nonpersistent and do not represent ParaBank behavior.

Q: Why was the JMeter load so small?
A: ParaBank is a shared public demo. Five threads and ten read-only samples demonstrated the workflow without creating heavy traffic or changing data.

Q: What does 0% JMeter errors prove?
A: It proves that JMeter recorded no errors across those ten samples under that run's conditions. It does not prove capacity, concurrency limits, scalability, an SLA, or production readiness.

Q: Why is the formal outcome PARTIAL rather than all PASS?
A: Keyboard interactions and the sampled contrast check passed, but inspected registration inputs lacked programmatic labels. Preserving PARTIAL accurately represents mixed accessibility evidence.

Q: Why include a simulated defect?
A: DEF-001 demonstrates a realistic whitespace-only input-validation risk. It is explicitly simulated, Medium severity, and was never executed or presented as observed.

Q: Are 22 formal cases, five UI methods, and four BDD instances the same kind of count?
A: No. Formal cases are requirements-oriented result records. UI methods are executable JUnit units. BDD instances are one Scenario plus three outline examples. Supporting executions are not added to the 22-case tally.
